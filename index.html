Improving Medication Administration Accuracy

A quality improvement project focused on reducing medication administration errors on an inpatient medical-surgical unit through standardized processes and multidisciplinary collaboration.

Overview Problem Aim & Objectives Methods Results Lessons & Sustainability Team Contact

Project Overview

Medication administration errors are a major patient safety concern and can increase morbidity, length of stay, and healthcare costs. This quality improvement (QI) initiative was designed to reduce medication administration errors on a 32-bed medical-surgical unit by applying a structured QI framework, improving processes, and engaging frontline staff.

Problem Statement & Background

Baseline data from January–March 2024 showed an average of 8 reported medication administration errors per 1,000 medication doses on the unit. The most common issues involved wrong time, missed doses, and documentation errors. Incident reports and staff feedback highlighted system-level barriers such as inconsistent workflows and BCMA device issues.

Aim & Objectives

SMART Aim

To reduce reported medication administration errors on the medical-surgical unit by 50% (from 8 to 4 per 1,000 doses) by December 31, 2024, while maintaining or improving staff satisfaction and reporting culture.

Objectives

  • Standardize the medication administration process across all shifts.
  • Improve adherence to barcode medication administration (BCMA) policies.
  • Enhance staff knowledge and confidence related to safe medication administration.
  • Promote a culture of safety and non-punitive error reporting.

Methods

QI Framework

This project utilized the Plan-Do-Study-Act (PDSA) cycle to test and refine interventions in small, rapid cycles before wider implementation.

Measures

  • Outcome: Medication administration errors per 1,000 doses.
  • Process: BCMA scan rate; on-time medication administration.
  • Balancing: Nurse-reported workload, satisfaction, and time per med pass.

Data Sources

  • Incident reporting system
  • Electronic health record (EHR)
  • Anonymous staff surveys

Key Interventions

  1. Standardized Medication Administration Checklist Co-developed with frontline nurses and incorporated into daily practice.
  2. BCMA Optimization Ensured functional scanners, addressed technical issues, and provided quick-reference guides.
  3. Targeted Education Sessions Brief, case-based in-services focusing on high-risk medications and common error modes.
  4. Visual Job Aids Posters and badge cards outlining the “5+2 Rights” and unit-specific safety tips.
  5. Data Feedback & Recognition Monthly safety huddles sharing updated metrics and recognizing improvement efforts.

Results

Medication Errors Decreased from 8 to 3.5 per 1,000 doses over 9 months.
BCMA Adherence Improved from 78% to 95% of doses scanned.
On-time Administration Improved from 65% to 82% within the 60-minute window.

Quantitative Findings

Run charts demonstrated a sustained downward shift in medication administration errors following implementation of the standardized checklist and BCMA optimization. Special cause variation was observed beginning in month 4, corresponding to combined interventions.

Qualitative Feedback

  • Nurses reported greater confidence in medication safety practices.
  • Perceived communication with pharmacy improved.
  • Increased near-miss reporting indicated a more open and proactive safety culture.

Lessons Learned & Sustainability

Lessons Learned

  • Early involvement of frontline staff in intervention design was critical for buy-in and adoption.
  • Addressing technical and workflow barriers (e.g., scanner availability) enabled behavior change.
  • Regular feedback loops and recognition helped maintain engagement and momentum.

Sustainability Plan

  • Embed the medication safety checklist into orientation for all new nurses.
  • Continue monthly safety huddles and visual display of run charts on the unit.
  • Spread successful interventions to additional units within the organization.
